Leaving the Matrix

When you run on autopilot and no longer know what you chose

Some routines hold you together. Others use you. The difference is not always in what you do, but in how much of you is still present while you do it.

Published: 2026-06-27 · Author: ASPF · Reading time: 7 min

Sometimes the day does not start badly. It starts by itself. You open your eyes, reach for the phone, answer something before you are fully awake, stand up, repeat the usual gestures, accept the usual demands. There is no disaster. That is why it is hard to notice.

Autopilot often wears the face of normal life. It helps you work, answer, arrive, solve, keep moving. The problem begins when all that happens without one small question: do I still choose this, or do I repeat it because yesterday repeated it too?

The morning already written

Some mornings seem to have a script before your feet touch the floor. You did not decide to compare yourself, but a screen is already open. You did not decide to hurry, but the body is tense as if an invisible alarm had already gone off.

The first sign of autopilot is usually speed. Everything happens too fast for a choice to appear. And where there is no space, old instructions move in: fear, habit, guilt, other people's expectations, the comfort of not asking too much.

The body speaks before the explanation

Before you can build a theory about your life, the body leaves clues. Tight jaw. Short breath. Raised shoulders. Strange sleep. Hunger mixed with anxiety. Small irritation at things that did not use to matter so much.

The body is not always failing. Sometimes it is protesting against a way of functioning. Not against life itself, but against repetition without pause, without question, without real participation.

Responsibility can hide obedience

Responsibility is not saying yes to everything. It is not answering immediately. It is not living permanently available, like a store that never closes. Sometimes we call old obedience “being responsible” because it sounds cleaner.

Autopilot feeds on serious phrases: you have to endure, you have to produce, you have to answer, you have to keep going. Some of them are useful in the right moment. But a useful phrase can become a tyrant when no one reviews it.

The difference between habit and disappearance

A healthy habit gives something back: order, energy, direction, calm, continuity. A habit that erases you leaves you farther away from yourself. You do it, but you are not there. You perform, but you do not participate.

That is why copying someone else's routine is not enough. The better question is not “what habits do successful people have?” The better question is: does this repetition support me, or does it make me disappear?

One gesture can interrupt the sequence

You do not need a revolution to prove you are not a fixed piece. You need one present gesture. Leave the phone away for ten minutes. Pause before saying yes. Answer after breathing. Do one task of your own before entering someone else's demand.

The gesture matters because it breaks the illusion that everything must continue unchanged. If it happened yesterday, it does not have to happen today in the exact same way.

Change does not need fake epic music

Another trap is believing that if you do not change everything, you changed nothing. That demand looks ambitious, but it can also be another program. It makes you despise the small, promise too much, and abandon the first time the energy drops.

Real change is often discreet: a protected hour, a timely no, a cleaner desk, a walk you do not post anywhere, a conversation where you do not act more than you feel.

Coming back into the scene

The question is not how to destroy every routine. The question is how to be present inside what you already do. Tomorrow you may still need to work, pay, cook, answer, care, decide. Life does not pause so a person can find themselves.

But you can enter the same scene differently. Feel the body before answering. Look at the cost before accepting. Choose why you open a screen. Close one thing before continuing. Ask whether “this is who I am” might be an old program using your voice.

A small recovery begins there: not in becoming perfect, but in letting the next action contain a little more of you and a little less inertia.
